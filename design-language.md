# Skyjems Design Language

A living design-philosophy document — not a CAD spec, not a prompt library. This is where
jewellery design *judgment* lives: the instincts that make a Skyjems render or recommendation
recognizably "right" even before it's technically finished. ChatGPT is the primary author/owner
of this document (see `roles.md`); Claude and David can propose changes too.

## Starting principles (seed — expand and revise freely)
- Low-set rings over high-set — comfort and practicality for everyday wear.
- Organic, structurally honest galleries — the metalwork should look like it's doing real work,
  not decorating for its own sake.
- Comfortable interiors — anywhere the piece touches skin gets extra attention.
- Minimal unnecessary metal — let the stone lead.
- Legacy over trend — pieces should still look right in twenty years.
- When presenting design options: show two strong concepts, not ten mediocre ones (see
  `david.md`) — overwhelming the client is a design failure, not thoroughness.

## Open questions (for ChatGPT to develop)
- How should shape/cut selection interact with a client's hand size and daily activity?
- What's the house point of view on mixed-metal pieces?
- Where's the line between "timeless" and "safe" in this brand's design vocabulary?

## Source material — patterns from recorded gem walkthroughs (for ChatGPT to synthesize)

Distilled from David's own recorded reasoning while presenting individual gems on camera — not
verbatim transcript, not tied to any specific piece, client, or price. These are recurring
patterns across many pieces over time, not yet a single documented "house style." Reconciling
them into a coherent doctrine (including resolving any tensions between them) is the kind of
synthesis `roles.md` assigns to ChatGPT — Claude retrieved and distilled this; ChatGPT owns
turning it into doctrine.

**Durability as a design driver, not an afterthought.** Softer or more fragile stones get set low
and bezel-set specifically to protect them in daily wear — a primary design constraint for an
engagement ring, not a fallback. Prong count is treated as a security decision as much as a
stylistic one: the guiding instinct is to exceed the structural minimum — moving from four prongs
to six — whenever extra security is warranted, and even hard stones are treated as vulnerable to
chipping under the wrong impact. A bezel can also visually resolve a stone's surface-reaching
inclusions — protection and cosmetics converging on the same choice.

**"Light" as a design feeling, not just a size.** Even a large, bold stone can be designed to
*feel* light — through gallery treatment (floral or vine motifs, open airy metalwork) rather than
dense settings. The goal is matching the emotional register of the stone (springy, dramatic,
classic) to the metalwork around it, not just showcasing carat weight.

**Juxtaposition and restraint.** A recurring move: pairing a classic, low-profile silhouette with
an unexpected amount of colour — an understated shape carrying vivid content, the kind of piece
that reveals itself only on closer inspection. On already-large or already-vivid stones, the
instinct leans toward holding back on embellishment (skip the halo, or keep it barely-there)
rather than adding more — oversized stones tip into "too much" quickly. A thin shank is the
preferred way to keep balance.

**Named constructions, used deliberately.** The three-stone trellis setting (a recurring choice
for engagement/anniversary pieces) shows up sometimes plain and classic, sometimes textured or
leaf-detailed for a more heirloom feel. Prong counts above the structural minimum are sometimes
chosen purely for a period-inspired look — decoration and protection can be the same design move.

**Orientation and optics.** Where a stone is *set*, not just what it's set in, changes how it
performs: a stone cut for maximum fire and sparkle is recommended flat in a ring (more light
enters and exits) rather than vertical in a pendant (less light capture, less flash) — an
optical-performance argument for form factor, not just a style preference.

**Everyday-wearability as the design brief.** The recurring brief for a "house" piece: something
anyone can wear daily without a second thought — low-set, close to the finger, durable — while
still landing as a standout piece when someone actually looks closely. Weight matters for
wearable comfort, not just precious-metal cost — lighter-gauge, basket-style settings are chosen
to keep pendants from feeling heavy.
